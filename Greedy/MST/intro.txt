Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) – Introduction

A Minimum Spanning Tree (MST) is a concept in graph theory where we connect all vertices of a weighted, undirected graph with the minimum possible total edge weight, without forming any cycles.

In other words, MST creates a tree that:

Connects all vertices

Contains V − 1 edges (where V is number of vertices)

Has no cycles

Has the minimum total edge cost

This problem is commonly solved using Greedy Algorithms, where we always choose the minimum available edge while building the tree.

Why MST is Important

Minimum Spanning Trees are used in many real-world problems such as:

Designing network cables between computers

Road construction between cities with minimum cost

Electrical grid design

Clustering in machine learning

The goal is always to connect everything with the least cost.

Algorithms to Find MST

There are two popular algorithms to find the MST:

Prim's Algorithm – Builds the MST by expanding from a starting vertex.

Kruskal's Algorithm – Builds the MST by selecting the smallest edges first.

Both algorithms follow the Greedy approach, but they work differently internally.
